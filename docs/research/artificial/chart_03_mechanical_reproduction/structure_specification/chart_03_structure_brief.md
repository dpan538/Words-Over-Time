# Chart 03 Structure Brief

## 1. Purpose of Chart 3

Chart 3 shows how `artificial` shifts from making substitute objects toward manufacturing perceptual experience itself. It is not a chart about mass media or technology history. It is a chart about the point where artificial stops replacing a thing and starts replacing an experience — and how that shift produces a second wave of authenticity anxiety.

Working thesis:

```text
Artificial moves from substituting objects
to manufacturing experience directly:
image, sound, light, scene.
Authenticity pressure arrives twice —
first as old inauthenticity vocabulary declining,
then as theoretical authenticity vocabulary rising
through the gap that reproduction left.
```

## 2. Relation to Chart 2

Chart 2 showed:

```text
artificial under semantic pressure from not-natural and not-real,
producing suspicion.
```

Chart 3 follows that suspicion into its historical substrate. It shows the language that built the pressure field: the vocabulary of spectacle, reproduction, and perceptual manufacture, and the authenticity counter-vocabulary that forms on both sides of the reproduction era.

Chart 3 is temporal. Chart 2 was spatial/geometric. They are companion structures.

## 3. Core Narrative Structure

The chart should make one argument visible:

```text
Five strata of vocabulary accumulate between 1800 and 2019.
Each stratum introduces a new technology of artificial experience.
The authenticity counter-vocabulary shows a valley shape:
old inauthenticity terms (imitation, forgery) decline through the reproduction era,
then new theoretical authenticity terms (authenticity, simulation) rise
on the far side of that valley.
```

The valley is the central visual argument. It must be visible.

## 4. Five Strata

The chart is organized as five horizontal bands, read from top to bottom. Each band holds a layer of vocabulary that addresses a distinct kind of artificial perceptual experience.

### Stratum I — Perceptual Quality Pressure

This is the authenticity / inauthenticity counter-vocabulary.
It exists above and across the whole chart because it frames the entire narrative.

This stratum shows the **two-wave structure**:
- Left side (1800–1880): old terms high and declining — `imitation`, `forgery`, `counterfeit`, `genuine`
- Middle (1880–1960): valley — these terms fade as reproduction rises; no new authenticity vocabulary has yet formed
- Right side (1960–2019): new terms rising — `authenticity`, `simulation`, `simulated`, `authentic experience`

The valley in the middle is the argument. Reproduction technology did not immediately produce authenticity anxiety; it temporarily erased the vocabulary for it.

Structural anchor terms for Stratum I:
- `imitation` — peak 1810, 18.69/M (old inauthenticity, left wave)
- `forgery` — peak 1830, 9.99/M (old inauthenticity, left wave)
- `genuine` — peak 1805, 22.48/M (old authenticity affirmation, left wave)
- `lifelike` — peak 1871, 0.48/M (quality-pressure bridge, mid-left)
- `true to life` — peak 1943, 0.32/M (quality-pressure bridge, mid)
- `authenticity` — peak 2016, 6.51/M (new theoretical authenticity, right wave)
- `simulation` — peak 1995, 36.14/M (new simulated-experience vocabulary, right wave)

### Stratum II — Spectacle Apparatus

Pre-cinema devices for manufactured visual experience. These are the first technologies of artificial perception, predating photography and cinema.

Structural anchor terms:
- `panorama` — peak 1808, 4.20/M
- `diorama` — peak 1851, 1.41/M
- `stereoscope` — peak 1858, 1.71/M
- `magic lantern` — peak 1869, 0.67/M
- `automaton` — crosses 1/M in 1821, peak 2003 (nineteenth-century presence only)

### Stratum III — Reproduction Technology

Photography, sound recording, electric light, cinema, radio — the infrastructure of reproducible experience.

Structural anchor terms:
- `electric light` — peak 1897, 21.86/M
- `photograph` — crosses 1/M in 1832, peak 2009 (long structural arc)
- `phonograph` — crosses 1/M in 1878, peak 1947
- `moving picture` — peak 1916, 4.36/M
- `cinematograph` — peak 1914, 1.42/M
- `artificial lighting` — peak 1925, 0.39/M (the artificial-perception phrase within this layer)
- `radio broadcasting` — crosses 1/M in 1929, peak 1942

### Stratum IV — Mass Experience

The era when reproduction becomes ambient, consumer, and broadcast. Television dominates.

Structural anchor terms:
- `television` — peak 1953, **53.75/M** (the highest single peak in the dataset)
- `color film` — peak 1954, 1.21/M
- `mass production` — peak 1945, 3.87/M
- `mass media` — crosses 1/M in 1956, peak 1990
- `high fidelity` — crosses 1/M in 1953, peak 1956, 2.86/M
- `recorded music` — peak 1952, 0.43/M

### Stratum V — Simulation and Digital Experience

The late-tail layer where artificial experience becomes computational. These terms rise exactly as the old reproduction-era vocabulary peaks and begins to decline.

Structural anchor terms:
- `computer graphics` — peak 1986, 2.57/M
- `digital image` — peak 1999, 1.21/M
- `virtual reality` — peak 2019, 2.54/M
- `simulation` — (appears also in Stratum I; double-coded as both new-authenticity and simulation-technology)
- `digital recording` — peak 1985, 0.42/M

## 5. Visual Form

The chart is a **stratigraphic timeline**.

```text
Time axis: horizontal, 1800 → 2019
Strata: five horizontal bands, stacked top to bottom
Term representation: each structural anchor term is a bell-curve arc
  positioned at its peak year on the horizontal axis,
  scaled vertically within its stratum by peak per-million value,
  rising and falling symmetrically or asymmetrically based on
  the era brackets (avg_1800_1849 through avg_1950_1999)
Visual weight: arc width encodes approximate era of high activity;
  arc height encodes peak frequency
```

The five strata share a common time axis but have independent vertical scales.
This is intentional: `television` at 53.75/M would flatten all other strata
if they shared a vertical scale.

## 6. The Valley as Central Visual

Stratum I (Perceptual Quality Pressure) must show the valley explicitly.

The old inauthenticity terms (imitation, forgery, genuine) form arcs clustered at the left side, 1800–1880. Their arcs are large and left-leaning.

The new authenticity terms (authenticity, simulation) form arcs at the right side, 1960–2019. Their arcs are large and right-leaning.

Between them, from approximately 1880 to 1960, there is a visible low region — the arc density drops. This middle emptiness is the argument.

Optional: a faint shaded region indicating the "authenticity vacuum" between the two waves. Or a thin horizontal annotation line labeled:

```text
reproduction era — authenticity vocabulary at minimum
```

## 7. Aesthetic

Consistent with Chart 2:

```text
black background
white arc linework
white labels, sparse
five stratum labels at left margin (small caps or italic)
time axis labels at bottom: 1800, 1850, 1900, 1950, 2000
no grid lines
no tick marks beyond axis labels
thin hairline stratum dividers (optional, or just spacing)
```

Label placement rule: term labels appear at peak position only, small, white.
If peaks cluster (e.g. 1950s), stagger labels vertically to avoid overlap.
Terms without a structural role in the argument are not labeled — they are present as thin unlabeled arcs at reduced opacity.

## 8. Interaction (Hover States)

Hover over a labeled arc:
- arc brightens / stroke weight increases
- small tooltip or inline label appears: term, peak year, peak per-million, stratum name

Hover over a stratum label (left margin):
- all arcs in that stratum highlight
- arcs in other strata dim to low opacity

Default: all structural anchor arcs at full opacity; texture/background arcs at 25–35% opacity.

## 12. What Chart 3 Must Not Become

Chart 3 must not become:

- a connected network graph
- a scatter plot with a single shared scale
- a timeline of technological inventions
- a history of media chart
- a word cloud
- a bar chart comparing peak values
- a chart about Walter Benjamin or critical theory
- a data dashboard with filters
- a chart that shows all 90 terms with equal weight

Chart 3 ends at the point where simulated experience is computationally manufactured and authenticity is theorized. It does not extend into AI-generated content, deepfakes, or synthetic media — those belong to a later chart.

# Round 02 Realistic Bridge Findings

## 1. Why realistic matters

`Realistic` is the clearest term that stops Chart 4B from collapsing into a simple real versus fake binary.
It gives us a middle zone:

- not real
- not necessarily fake
- convincingly resembling the real

That is exactly the kind of bridge a semantic mobility model needs.

## 2. Realistic artificial flowers / teeth / skin / voice evidence

The bridge evidence is now spread across several object and process types:

- `realistic artificial skin` is the strongest and cleanest example
- `realistic fake teeth` and artificial teeth examples support body replacement
- `realistic artificial eyes` supports display and representational realism
- `realistic artificial voice` extends the bridge from objects to human-like process
- realistic artificial plants show the bridge in everyday consumer language

## 3. Why realistic is not the same as real

Every strong example from this round depends on the distinction.
The object or process is artificial, but its appearance, sound, or effect is realistic.
So `realistic` measures quality of resemblance, not ontological status.

## 4. Whether realistic can be a bridge basin

Yes.
Round 01 suggested this.
Round 02 strengthens it enough that `realistic` now deserves treatment as a genuine bridge basin or attractor field, especially when paired with `simulated`.

The main caution is source balance.
Many current examples come from product or explanatory sources rather than literary or historical corpora.
